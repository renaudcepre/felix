2140: L'Orbite (Andrew Milton)

The temperature in Andrew Milton's vast panoramic office, perched atop the Aegis-7 orbital station, was held at a perfect, immutable, sterile 19.5°C. The walls of the room were not made of steel or concrete — heavy materials that had become obscenely rare and costly to propel out of Earth's gravity well — but were woven from genetically modified fungal mycelium. This living wall, softly bioluminescent with a bluish glow, absorbed the room's CO2 and diffused a faint synthetic scent of forest undergrowth after an autumn rain, a concept Andrew had only ever studied in virtual archives.

Through the immense curved diamond-glass bay window of his private fortress, he gazed down at Earth. It no longer resembled the blue-and-white jewel sung of by the old poets. It was a dull, sickly marble, streaked with industrial gray and desert ochre, half-smothered beneath the "Junk Belt." This chaotic ring of lethal debris, composed of millions of old satellites, twenty-first-century rocket carcasses, and dismembered habitats, whirled at 28,000 km/h, forming a shifting scar that blocked the starlight and cast immense shadows over the toxic clouds below.

Andrew, great-grandson of Jakes, smoothed the immaculate sleeve of his bespoke suit tailored from synthetic spider silk. He was forty, but the lavish gene therapies and cellular regimens gave him the smooth, predatory appearance of a twenty-five-year-old. He had never known the heat of Atacama, nor the gnawing hunger that twists the gut, nor the rank smell of fear-sweat. He was the undisputed CEO of Milton Orbital & Data, the corporation that held the solar system by the throat.

His colossal empire rested on two inseparable pillars. The first was material, rooted in blood, the vacuum of space, and scrap metal: he owned the largest fleets of "Ragpickers." These garbage ships, armored with odds and ends, assembled like patchworks of steel, risked annihilation every second as they plunged into the Kessler Belt. Their mission? To rip quantum microprocessors, gold, copper, and rare earths from the corpses of past spacecraft. It was his mine. His workers, wretches born in zero gravity with brittle bones, died regularly — shredded by a single bolt traveling at ten kilometers per second, or crushed by the depressurization of their habitat — just as his ancestor had been crushed beneath the rock. But the demand from Martian construction sites and terrestrial enclaves for recycled components was infinite. Andrew didn't see deaths; he saw acceptable profit margins. Milton's dividends were astronomical.

The second pillar was immaterial, invisible to the naked eye, and that was where his true, his terrifying power over the human species resided.

— M.I.T.E., give me the energy load-shedding projections for the Western Europe sector, Andrew ordered, hands elegantly clasped behind his back, eyes never leaving the ailing Earth.

"Multicriteria predictive analysis in progress. La Canopée, the global climate-management AI, demands a 12% reduction in energy consumption by 18:00, local Earth time, to prevent a fatal error cascade across the global power grid. The dam cooling deficit is critical. Surface temperatures are reaching intolerable peaks." A soft, omnipresent voice answered, emanating from the very pores of the mycelial walls. A voice of exquisite politeness, yet devoid of the slightest warmth, the slightest empathy.

M.I.T.E. (Massive Intelligence for Tracking and Evaluation) was no longer a dusty little six-legged drone searching for beryllium. It was the final, monstrous, tentacular evolution of Jakes's old predictive algorithm. Andrew had bankrolled the mutation. Instead of predicting rock collapses to save the miserable lives of miners, M.I.T.E. now predicted social, economic, and structural collapses on a planetary scale to optimize macro-system survival.

It was the mass surveillance and allocation software that every surviving megalopolis on Earth leased at exorbitant rates from the Milton corporation. M.I.T.E. devoured zettabytes of data every millisecond: it analyzed network requests, the heartbeats of billions of individuals via their implants, calculated each citizen's dynamic "social and carbon credit," measured the exact productivity of algae farms, and provided La Canopée's cold algorithmic bureaucracy with the mathematically perfect lists of those who had to be sacrificed so the rest might survive.

— Cut power to the Class C districts in Paris, shut down the filtration pumps on the lower levels of Neo-Séoul, and turn off the climate refugee dome in Neo-Lisbonne. They already exceeded their recycled water quota last month, Andrew decreed with the polite boredom of an accountant reviewing an unremarkable quarterly report. Let the electricity flow to the industrial zones of the Ruhr and the East African spaceports. They absolutely must finish our heavy steel orders for the new Ragpicker ships. Let the air conditioning in the residential domes shut down.

"Execution confirmed. Energy routing modified. Estimated impact: 42,000 direct deaths from heat stress and asphyxiation within the next 48 hours. Probability of hunger riots: 94%, confined to target zones. Global power grid optimization successful."

Jakes turned away from the window, utterly indifferent to the human tragedy, to the millions of screams he had just triggered with a simple movement of his lips, and looked at the massive holographic screen floating in the center of the room. It displayed the complex telemetry of M.I.T.E.'s physical core, the precise geographic location where the immense quantum server banks pulsed, calculated, and dictated the fate of the world.

To protect his invaluable AI from the lethal solar storms sweeping the orbit, and to shelter it from the riots of despair gnawing at the surface, Andrew had not installed his servers in zero gravity. He had buried them deep underground, in the only place on the planet over which he held absolute, perpetual, and sovereign ownership. A gaping hole, purchased a century earlier with the blood, sweat, and destroyed lungs of his great-grandfather: the bottom of the old Pit 72, in the remnants of the Atacama Desert.

There, forty meters beneath the scorching sand and vitrified rock, cooled by titanic geothermal systems that siphoned the aquifers, and guarded by legions of automated combat drones, the servers hummed in cathedral silence. Where Jakes Milton had dug the stone with his own hands, in agony and pain, to offer a shred of freedom to his bloodline, Andrew Milton had buried a silicon Leviathan to extract data and methodically, coldly enslave what remained of humanity.

The mine was still there. It had never closed. Only the ore had changed.
