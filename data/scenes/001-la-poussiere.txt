2055: La Poussière (Jakes Milton)

The analog thermometer, set into the scratched visor of Jakes's suit, wavered before displaying 54°C in the shadow of the excavator. But that shadow was nothing more than an illusion of respite, a mere variation in the intensity of the torment. At the bottom of Pit 72, a gaping artificial crater torn from the crust of what was once the Atacama Desert, the air itself seemed to have a texture. It boiled. There were no more seasons since the Great Climate Retreat, just a blinding, white, hostile sun and this red dust. Fine as talcum powder, it infiltrated with an almost organic malice: into the theoretically sealed joints of the suits, into the respiratory micro-filters that wheezed in agony, all the way into the metallic, coppery taste of his own saliva.

Jakes Milton coughed violently, a wet cough that tore through his chest. He spat a dark glob against the rock wall and tapped the scratched polycarbonate dome of "Mite," his class-4 assistance drone. Mite was a stocky machine, a worn-out hexapod bristling with grimy sensors and squealing servos, driven by a primitive Artificial Intelligence. It was nothing more than a simple predictive algorithm, a local neural network designed to analyze the micro-vibrations of the rock, anticipate pockets of toxic gas, and — theoretically — prevent independent miners, the cannon fodder of this new gold rush, from ending up crushed under tons of basalt.

— Tell me you've got something, tin can, Jakes growled, his voice scraped raw by the silica particles coating his throat with every breath. The primary airlock's cooling system just gave out. We've got no water left for the suit's radiators. Another twenty minutes and my blood's going to literally start boiling.

The drone pivoted slowly on its articulated legs, struggling against the pit's gravity. It scanned the sheer wall with a greenish topographic laser that strained to cut through the air saturated with suspended dust. The device emitted a series of mechanical clicks as it cross-referenced its seismic data with the megacorporation's geological archives.

"Analysis complete. Beryllium deposit probability: 87%. Estimated depth: 42 meters. Warning: Structural density of surrounding rock assessed as critical. Risk of bell-shaped collapse: High." Mite's synthetic voice crackled in Jakes's earpiece, monotone and devoid of emotion.

Jakes smiled wide enough to crack his chapped lips, deliberately ignoring the warning about the instability. Beryllium. It was the ultimate Grail. Down below, on the rest of the Earth, the world was dying of thirst, ecosystems were collapsing like houses of cards, and the water wars were ravaging the last habitable continents. But up above, in the rarefied, climate-controlled air of boardrooms, the megacorporations were no longer looking down. They were launching the "Grand Priming." They had a sickly, voracious need for ultra-light, heat-resistant metals to build the first electromagnetic cannons — the titanic SpinLaunch slings erected on the equator — designed to hurl automated factories toward the dead dust of Mars.

This vein wouldn't save Earth's biosphere, and Jakes couldn't care less. Earth was already a corpse. But a few kilos of pure beryllium would be enough to pay for a way out for his son, Elias, currently stuck in the stifling, flooded shanty towns of Neo-Santiago. A seat in the new corporate orbital academies. Far from this furnace, where the air was filtered down to the molecule and where water ran clear.

Jakes placed his gloved hand on the wall of Pit 72. Through the thick polymer layer, he could feel the rock vibrating, scorching, coursed through by the distant shockwaves of the other drills devouring the planet.
— We're digging, Mite. Disable the thermal safety protocols. Push the drilling actuators to 120%. And save the seismic, topographic, and legal data to your armored core. Triple redundancy copy. If something happens to me, if the mountain decides to close in on my head, make sure the smart inheritance contract activates instantly on the global network. Elias must get the exclusive extraction rights. That's his ticket. That's his legacy.

Two weeks later, Pit 72 gave way. It collapsed with a muffled roar, a terrifying tectonic spasm, swallowing Jakes under four hundred tons of barren rock and twisted metal. He died in absolute darkness, lungs full of blood and dust, bones crushed by geological pressure.

But Mite, the battered little drone, wedged into a miraculous crevice barely larger than its frame, had survived. For months, in the total, freezing darkness of its master's tomb, its emergency beacon blinked faintly. A small red eye in the abyss, continuously broadcasting the unalterable digital property title. That simple piece of code would go on to found, decades later, the all-powerful, the untouchable Milton dynasty.
