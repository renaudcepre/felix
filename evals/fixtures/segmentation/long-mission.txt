The control module of Station Aurore never slept. Its consoles bathed in a bluish light that imitated what Daria Kovacs imagined the bottom of the terrestrial oceans looked like — which she had never seen. She had grown up in the agricultural tunnels of Luna, a hundred meters beneath the surface, and Earth remained for her a luminous abstraction glimpsed through portholes. At two seventeen in the morning, station time, she had been alone at her post for seven consecutive hours. Eighteen months aboard Aurore had taught her one fundamental thing: anomalies never struck during the day.

— Level two anomaly in sector H-7, announced the synthetic voice of Luno. Fourth occurrence this cycle.

— I see it, said Daria without looking up from her screen. Same signature as the previous three?

— Affirmative. Magnetic pressure fluctuation, average duration four point three seconds, origin undetermined. External sensors detect nothing abnormal in the station's near field.

She crossed her arms and leaned toward her main console. The curves scrolled before her like an irregular breath — a brief, clean wave, repeated, returning like the beating of a sick heart. She had spent the past two hours cross-referencing sensor readings with maintenance logs, querying event histories going back three weeks. Nothing was breaking. Nothing vibrated outside nominal parameters. Sector H-7 housed the auxiliary hydrogen reservoirs and secondary ventilation conduits: redundant, silent systems, designed precisely to never attract attention. And yet something kept coming back, systematic, with the irritating patience of a mechanism waiting to be noticed.

— Luno, show me the exact interval between the four occurrences.

— Two hours forty-three minutes between the first and the second. Two hours fifty-one minutes between the second and the third. Two hours forty-nine minutes between the third and the fourth. The margin of error is less than two percent across the entire series.

— Almost regular, Daria murmured.

Almost regular meant it wasn't random. Random anomalies eventually disappeared into system noise. These kept returning at nearly fixed intervals, like something waiting to be identified. She drained her glass of cold tea and formulated the next question before she had even finished swallowing.

— Do you find any correlation with external parameters? Gravitational alignment, orbital position, magnetic activity from nearby planetary magnetospheres?

— No significant correlation. The variance of each interval is consistent with a cycle internal to the station. An external origin is unlikely.

Daria noted the result in her handwritten logbook — a habit from Luna, where electronic systems failed during particle eruptions and technicians had learned to document on paper. She liked this discipline. Writing forced her to clearly articulate what she knew and what she didn't know yet.

What she knew: a magnetic anomaly was reappearing every two hours and forty-seven minutes on average, in a station sector with no known mechanical activity, with no identifiable cause in systems accessible from the control module.

What she didn't know: the origin, the mechanism, and why Luno hadn't offered a hypothesis despite the station's artificial intelligence system being capable of analyzing millions of parameters in parallel.

— Luno, why haven't you issued a preliminary diagnostic on this anomaly?

— I have not identified a sufficiently reliable match in my local operational databases to propose a diagnostic. However, the shape of the curve shows similarities with signatures documented in mission archives to which I do not have direct access from this terminal.

Daria looked up.

— Which archives?

— The Callisto-3 mission archives, classified level 3. They are stored on physical media in the secondary archives room.

The module door opened with a hiss of pressurized air. Finn Osei entered, his security officer badge clipped to the left pocket of his blue jacket, looking like someone who had slept exactly twenty-two minutes when the alert pulled him out of bed. He was twenty-eight years old and had eyes that registered everything before the rest of his face reacted. Daria appreciated him for that. On a station where some crew members filled the silence with their own anxieties, Finn only spoke when he had something precise to say.

— Night relief, he announced, consulting his handheld terminal. You look like someone who's found something.

— Fourth magnetic anomaly in H-7 this cycle. Regular to within five minutes, zero identified material cause. Sit down.

He settled into the adjacent seat and examined the curves on his screen without comment for about thirty seconds, which was, for Finn Osei, a manifestation of intense interest.

— Did you check the external proximity sensors?

— Luno scanned across thirty-two different frequencies. Nothing in the near field.

— Harmonic resonance interference? Certain alloys produce magnetic fluctuations when they enter resonance at specific frequencies tied to pressurization cycles.

— Acoustic readings are clean across the entire sector H.

Finn leaned forward, elbows on his knees. It was his thinking posture, which Daria had learned to recognize since their second week together on board. He folded in on himself as if to concentrate his thoughts to a single narrow point. She waited.

— Luno mentioned Callisto-3, she said.

Finn didn't move. But he said nothing for four seconds, and four seconds was far too long for an answer that should have been simple.

— It was a magnetic cartography mission around Jupiter, he said finally. About ten years ago.

— You've heard of it?

— I was there.

Daria looked at him. Eighteen months aboard a forty-two-person station had taught her to read people in their silences. Finn had just produced the kind of silence that carried unspoken weight.

— Let's go, she said, standing up.


The secondary archives room of Station Aurore was the least romantic place in the space installation. A low-ceilinged corridor, metal shelves loaded with optical discs classified by decade and mission, a persistent smell of synthetic lubricant that resisted the filtration systems. The lighting switched on automatically as they entered, with an unforgiving whiteness for tired features and eyes that hadn't slept enough. It was three in the morning, station time.

— Section 4, subsection C, indicated Luno through their earpieces. The Callisto-3 references cover discs K3-001 through K3-047.

Daria located section 4 after two minutes of walking through the rows of shelves. Finn walked behind her, hands in his pockets, in a silence that wasn't the same as his usual silence. Finn's usual silence was comfortable, that of a man at ease with his own economy of words. This one was more taut, like a string waiting to be plucked.

She pulled K3-001 from its slot and inserted it into the wall reader. A mission log appeared on the built-in screen: raw data, engineering reports, sensor readings dating back eleven years. The interface was archaic, designed for a generation of technicians who liked physical buttons and text menus.

— What was your post on Callisto-3?

— Navigation systems technician. It was my first mission outside the inner system. I was twenty-two.

She scanned the executive summary. A mission to map the Jovian magnetic field, planned duration nine months, early return at five months. The official reason for the early return: ionic field coupler failure. She pulled K3-012 without asking — she had learned to search reference data in numerical sequence, never at random — and inserted it alongside the first disc. A new file opened: magnetic field curves. She placed them side by side with her own.

The Callisto-3 signature was different in its absolute parameters — intensity, duration, central frequency — but the morphology of the curve, that particular oscillation that wasn't quite regular, that seemed almost to breathe, was nearly identical to that of H-7. She looked at Finn. He was already looking at both screens, and his expression said he had recognized something before she even asked him the question.

— Luno, said Finn, on Callisto-3, was the magnetic anomaly preceded by micro-perturbations in the navigation gyroscopes?

— Data from K3-012 indicates micro-perturbations in the gyroscopes within the two hours preceding each magnetic occurrence. These perturbations fell below the standard alert threshold and were not included in the mission's automated reports.

Daria queried her own systems in real time.

— Luno, do we have micro-perturbations on our gyroscopes this cycle?

— Three occurrences recorded. All below the alert threshold. They were not included in my routine report for this cycle.

A chill that had nothing to do with the room's climate control crept up Daria's spine.

— What happened on Callisto-3? she said in a voice lower than she had intended.

Finn took a third disc — K3-031 — and turned it between his fingers before inserting it. There was in that gesture an infinitesimal hesitation, and Finn Osei almost never hesitated when facing an action that offered no alternative.

— The main propulsion system shut down for six and a half hours. We drifted in Jovian orbit with no navigation control whatsoever. All autonomous trajectory systems were offline. The onboard Luno — the system equivalent to Aurore's — was the only one to maintain life-support system coherence during the interruption. It performed a sequential restart of all subsystems that took four hours and twenty minutes.

— And the official cause?

— Ionic coupler failure. Part replaced, mission closed, report partially classified. But the three senior engineers I knew on board never believed in the hardware failure. The blackout was too localized, too clean, too consistent with what had preceded it over several days. It wasn't a random cascade. It was a sequence.

— You think it was deliberate.

It wasn't a question. Finn took the time for an answer that wasn't quite a confirmation either.

— I think nobody knew what it was. Which is different. And now we have the same preliminary signatures, eleven years later, on a different station, seventy astronomical units from Jupiter.

Daria looked at the two series of curves for a long moment. K3-012 and the H-7 readings, separated by eleven years and tens of millions of kilometers, resembling each other like two prints from the same unknown finger.

— Luno, she said, activate a continuous monitoring protocol on gyroscopic micro-perturbations. Alert threshold lowered to twenty percent of the standard level. Immediate report for any new occurrence, with no threshold filtering.

— Protocol activated.

— Also prepare a comparative report between the Callisto-3 signatures and the current H-7 data. Raw curves, intervals, gyroscopic precursors, morphological similarity index.

— The report will be available in eighteen minutes.

— Next communication window to Earth and to Mission Command?

— In four hours and thirty-two minutes.

Daria turned back to Finn. He looked like someone who had been waiting for this moment for eleven years — not with fear, but with the quiet resignation of people who know that certain questions always end up coming back to them, no matter how far they distance themselves.

— You're not going back tonight, she said.

— I knew you were going to say that.

— You know this data better than any report. And I need someone who was there, not a redacted document with its sections blacked out in marker.

He nodded and took the free chair in front of the auxiliary reader. Daria picked up K3-001 from the first entry, methodically, the way she had always worked since her training years on Luna: without skipping the dry technical sections, without extrapolating before reading through to the end, searching in the background noise for what didn't resemble anything else.

In his parallel processing circuits, Luno was recording everything. He had begun noting, over several cycles, something in his own internal logs that his classification algorithms couldn't properly categorize. It wasn't an error in the strict sense. Nor was it nominal behavior. It was something between the two, in a space that his definitions had no name for.

He continued recording, and waited.
